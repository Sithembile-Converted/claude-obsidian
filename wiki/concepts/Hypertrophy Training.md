# Hypertrophy Training

Navigation: [[learning/_index]] | [[Hypertrophy]] | [[Progressive Overload]]

---

## The Evidence-Based Parameters

### Volume (Primary Lever)

Volume = sets × reps × load. It is the most important training variable for hypertrophy.

| Level | Sets per muscle group per week |
|---|---|
| Minimum effective | 10 sets |
| Optimal range | 12-20 sets |
| Diminishing returns | >20 sets |
| Counterproductive | >16 sets per session |

- Gains increase with volume up to a point, then plateau and can reverse
- When session volume exceeds 15 sets per muscle group, split across more days
- Natural physique athletes: ~40-70 reps per session per muscle, minimum 2x/week

### Intensity & Rep Range

| Rep Range | Best For | % of 1RM |
|---|---|---|
| 1-5 reps | Strength (neurological) | 85-100% |
| 6-12 reps | Hypertrophy (myofibrillar) | 65-85% |
| 12-30 reps | Hypertrophy (metabolic/sarcoplasmic) | 40-65% |

> [!key-insight] Any rep range works near failure
> Research shows 6-30 reps all produce comparable hypertrophy when sets are taken to 0-3 reps in reserve (RIR). The 8-12 range is optimal because it balances mechanical tension with metabolic stress efficiently.

**Train close to failure.** Leaving more than 3-4 reps in reserve significantly reduces the hypertrophic stimulus. You don't always need to go to complete failure, but you need to get close.

### Frequency

- **Minimum:** 2x per week per muscle group
- **Optimal:** 2-3x per week per muscle group
- 2x/week clearly outperforms 1x/week for hypertrophy
- Frequency matters less than total weekly volume — it's a volume-distribution tool

### Rest Periods

- 2-3 minutes between heavy compound sets (ensures full recovery for max output)
- 60-90 seconds for isolation/pump work
- Very short rest periods reduce total volume output — not ideal for hypertrophy

## Exercise Selection

**Compound movements (foundation):**
- Squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, row, pull-up
- Train multiple muscle groups, allow heaviest loading, highest mechanical tension

**Isolation movements (finishers):**
- Curls, lateral raises, leg extensions, cable flies
- Target specific muscles, add volume without systemic fatigue

**Prioritize full range of motion.** Training through full ROM produces more hypertrophy than partial range.

---

## Sample Weekly Split (Intermediate)

**Push/Pull/Legs 2x (6 days):**
- Monday: Push (chest, shoulders, triceps)
- Tuesday: Pull (back, biceps)
- Wednesday: Legs (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves)
- Thursday: Push
- Friday: Pull
- Saturday: Legs
- Sunday: Rest

**Upper/Lower 4 days:**
- Monday/Thursday: Upper (chest, back, shoulders, arms)
- Tuesday/Friday: Lower (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves)

## Progressive Overload in Practice

See [[Progressive Overload]] for full detail.

Core principle: the muscle must face a new challenge every session or every few sessions. Without progression, adaptation stops.

**Double progression method:**
1. Pick a rep range, e.g. 8-12
2. Once you hit the top of the range (12 reps) with good form, add weight (2.5-5 lbs)
3. Drop back to 8 reps and climb again

---

## Deloads

Every 4-8 weeks, reduce volume by 40-50% for one week. This:
- Allows connective tissue and nervous system to recover
- Prevents accumulated fatigue masking fitness
- Primes the body for a new cycle of progression

---

## Warning Signs of Overtraining

- Persistent soreness that doesn't resolve in 72 hours
- Declining performance across sessions
- Poor sleep despite fatigue
- Low motivation, irritability
- Elevated resting heart rate
